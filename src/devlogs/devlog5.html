Devlog 5: Pivot & EDGE

Third Playtest & Level Rework

We conducted our third playtest during this phase, focusing on the reworked level design. Initially, our levels were linear, focusing on guiding player progression. However, we found that this linearity was a bit stifling and didn’t fit our new community-centered theme very well. So we devised a new level design, with a community center for rescued slimes and levels that spoke out from the community center.

This design, however, confused playtesters more than it was worth for our theme, so we revisited a linear format, making sure to use clearer design that focuses both on guided progression and intermittent challenge for a better experience. Our level designer learned that levels for our game should be made only as big as it needs to be to progress the player in the amount intended. Otherwise it feels troublesome to navigate, wasting the player's time.

EDGE Showcase

We showcased our game at the Experiential Development & Games Expo (EDGE) hosted by the School of Interactive Games and Media (IGM)! Overall it went well, with many playtesters enjoying the game and understanding how to progress. Our main issue for EDGE was in the preparation for it. Although we ran through the game and made a build beforehand, we ran into a number of issues during setup, resulting in approximately 16 extra builds, with the last one being done shortly after the event started. From this, we know to more thoroughly test our builds the day before, going beyond a cursory glance through. We were lucky enough that it didn’t get in the way of the event this time, but you can’t rely on that.

An Unexpected Change

One of our more unexpected changes was to cut the player’s ability to fall. During playtests, players would attempt to fall (e.g., from a ledge) and get stuck on the level’s geometry. Since the level design didn’t necessitate falling and it caused more issues than it solved, we removed the ability to fall. We saw a noticeable improvement in playability during playtests after. If gameplay doesn’t quite feel right, closely inspect if some of your naturally assumed mechanics are really necessary (and whether cutting them out is worth the player’s shock/disappointment when they find they can’t do it).

Team Bonding
